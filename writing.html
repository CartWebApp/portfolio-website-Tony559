Everett Dirksen Essay

Similar to the way the main characters in the movie “The Wizard of Oz” are connected by the “yellow brick road,” Americans are connected by commonalities. These commonalities of course being the rights granted by the constitution to almost all Americans. Almost all Americans, because there were still issues with civil rights in 1964, such as the Jim Crow laws that would encourage segregation and there was still rampant violence on African Americans. At this time, the United States House of Representatives passed a civil rights bill, which would essentially ban discrimination based on race, color, sex, religion, and national origin. However, a group of senators opposed the bill by using a stalling tactic to let the vote go unfinished. This group of senators were mostly from the southern states, with many being democrats. Everett Dirksen, a Republican US Senator, delivered a speech to his fellow senators on June 10, 1964 to enact cloture on the civil rights bill, instantly forcing the other senators to vote. Dirksen knows the importance in passing this bill, and how it would be a great milestone for the United States. He had the conviction that passing this bill was the most moral action that he could provide at the time. He also has the power to potentially bring this change because of his status as a U.S. senator who has a great knowledge of the history of the United States and the changes that have been made to it thus far. Dirksen attempts to persuade the other senators to enact cloture on a good bill that would otherwise stop progress for the United States by using allusions to notable historical figures that held a role in politics and through the use of epistrophe.

Dirksen utilizes allusions of notable historical figures who have held powerful positions in politics to provide himself a sense of credibility. Dirksen directly quotes these figures, calling them by name, “a great democrat, Jefferson by name, wrote the following words…,” while also mentioning a more well-known president, “as Lincoln once observed… the occasion is piled high with difficulty…” Nevertheless, allusions directly correspond to credibility in the human mind. Allusions give this desired credibility because the figure that is being alluded to often holds a high or notable status. This status is the single provider of the sought-after credibility. If the figure did not hold status and was essentially irrelevant, then they would hold little to no power in their name. The speaker or author then uses this powerful name as a backbone to their argument. The author or speaker normally uses a figure that their target audience recognizes and looks up to, so that the speaker and listener are brought to a common ground in which they can discuss topics at an equal level. Dirksen chose to allude to both of these men for a great reason; both of these men were in separate parties, and based on the intent of their previous statements, would more than likely support Dirksen’s argument in a heartbeat. This would work for Dirksen since the senators he is trying to convince are most likely southern democrats and skeptics in his own party, and by giving himself credibility through the use of these historical figures, he can give these senators his message. He believes that passing this good bill now will shape America into the place that it was truly meant to be, a place of freedom, freedom without discrimination. Stalling this bill would only prolong the inevitable change that would come to the United States, and it would be in the nation's best interest to do the morally correct action when change was running rampant because if they didn’t, the government would be met with opposition and backlash from many citizens. This stalling would also stall the United States as a whole, preventing progress in other aspects. As a senator of the United States, he knew that he could bring the change that the U.S. needed so desperately. Dirksen is not naive though, and knows that this simple speech will not single handedly turn the tide of opinions, so he hopes others will join in to help him in advocating for this act of cloture on the civil rights bill.

He also uses epistrophe to further strengthen his argument, and in doing so, brings a component of logicality. Dirksen lists off parts of America that connect it to itself, such as the railroads, automobiles, a common language, an equal tax pattern to white and nonwhites, air transportation, and accommodations laws, repeating that these parts “make it so” that the United States is integrated together to be one nation. On the topic of repetition, epistrophe is exactly that, in which a phrase of connected words is repeated at the end of a sentence. Since epistrophe is a more narrow form of repetition, it can be assumed that many things that apply to repetition apply to epistrophe. This includes the fact that by repeating a phrase, it is reinforced in the human mind, making it stronger and more important to this same human mind. This repetition of “makes it so” after listing the ways that the United States of America is integrated is perfect for Dirksen. This is also because his use of this phrase also brings commonality to his structure. The senators who are opposed to the bill are especially susceptible to this commonality in how America is integrated because they were alive when the United States was changing, they were there for when it grew larger and how it progressed. Knowing this, they would have to succumb to his reasoning of how this good bill would progress the U.S. even further at this time of change. He is calling out their hypocrisy in changing the United States in the past but not now, asking them what they are so afraid of since they have already experienced change. Since he repeatedly calls out their hypocrisy, the senators are forced to think of their decisions, with the added importance of knowing that they are public figures who are going against their own word. Dirksen had the ability to do this because of his position as a senator and as a man who knows much about the history of the United States. Ultimately, this would potentially end in the southern senators being convinced that the cloture of the bill is the logically correct action to do.

This speech is especially relevant in recent times because there are events happening around the world in which civil rights are being denied. However, this could have still been happening in the United States if this bill was not passed. Dirksen knew that had to be passed for America to progress further and for time to move forward.

Letter From a Birmingham Jail Essay

Melanin, a simple amino acid with a simple use, which is to add pigmentation to human skin, hair, and the iris of the eye, holds a strong presence in human history. Surprisingly, instead of understanding that melanin’s use is purely for the use of pigmentation, humanity has discriminated against itself based on the acid. African Americans, a group of people that have a higher amount of melanin, or rather, have a darker skin pigmentation, have been held as slaves for eighty-six years in the United States. Fortunately, they were set free in the year eighteen sixty-five. Unfortunately, however, the people were still discriminated against. Laws such as Jim Crow were enforced to segregate African Americans– they had separate drinking fountains, separate seats, separate schools, and separate stores. The schools allocated for them were often underfunded and cared less for. This discrimination led up to such atrocities as being unnecessarily condemned for crimes they did not commit, being the target of hate crimes, and even being the target of extreme violence. They had to live in fear every single day, with wives wondering if their husbands would come home that day or if they were murdered purely because of the color of their skin. In the nineteen sixties and slightly before, the impassioned African Americans banded together and started civil rights movements to fight for their freedoms. During these difficult times, the Southern Christian Leadership Conference was formed by brave black leaders. Birmingham, a city in Alabama, was extremely racially segregated, and in 1963, a black man was thrown in jail for leading a nonviolent protest only after he attempted to address the matter in court and through the legal systems. This man is Dr. Martin Luther King Jr, a black activist for the civil rights movement and a leader of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference. King was a reverend for his church and led multiple movements, such as the Montgomery Bus Boycott, with which he gained a great reputation. While he was in jail from the peaceful, nonviolent protest in Birmingham, he wrote a response, the Letter From Birmingham Jail, to eight white clergymen who denounced his actions in protesting. He wrote this letter to educate them about how his actions were neither untimely nor unwise. King attempts to educate these clergymen by referencing Adolf Hitler and through the use of biblical allusions.

King attempts to educate the eight clergymen by referencing one of the world's most evil men, Adolf Hitler. Dr. King attempted to logically educate them, comparing the topic of how, “Everything Adolf Hitler did in Germany was ‘legal,’’” and why the topic that it was illegal to, “aid and comfort a Jew in Hitler’s Germany” are similar to the events and atrocities that are happening in his own time. In human minds, comprehension stems from simplification. To arrive at this simple form of a topic, a strategy must be employed. One extremely effective method to bring the mind from point A to point B is by using a comparison of two alike topics. By providing a comparison of the atrocities that Hitler committed that were considered “legal,” King builds comprehension that even though something is legal, the legality does not equate to morality. Since the clergymen are white and should already have been educated due to their status, it can be assumed that they have the ability to understand how King uses the atrocities of Hitler toward Jews and compares them to how the law treats African Americans, further establishing how legality does not have a one-hundred-percent positive correlation with morality. Since they have this ability, King rides on the hope that they will be educated enough to potentially realize at least a part of their own wrongs. King decides to educate these clergymen due to their high role in their community, meaning they have more of a voice than others. King specifically targets the clergymen because they should theoretically care the most out of everyone except the black people since their religion commands them to do so. They clergymen will be forced to care, otherwise they would be hypocrites in their religion, preaching one thing and doing the opposite. As a result, if King manages to educate the clergymen, they may finally give him a chance and at least partially understand that his actions were not only timely but necessary as well.

Reverend King utilizes biblical allusions to build credibility in his argument and further level the playing field with the eight, white clergymen. King references God, giving an argument that he is similar to the prophets in eighth century B.C. in the way that they, “left their villages and carried their ‘thus saith the Lord’ far beyond the boundaries of their home towns,” and that he is like the biblical figure Paul in the way that he “must constantly respond to the Macedonian call for aid.” In doing so, he gives clear and undeniable evidence that his actions are supported by their lord. Additionally, it is known that religion is one of the highest forms of connection a human can have in their mind. Religion brings humans together. Furthermore, most religions include an important factor, that factor being faith. With this faith, there comes trust, since absolute faith in one thing disables the human from believing anything other than that one thing completely. This complete trust provides a sense of security and credibility in the human mind. King utilizes the knowledge that the eight clergymen were in the same religion as his own to bestow upon himself a deal of credibility and equal ground to talk because their entire being as clergymen is to follow the word of God, which he claims to carry. It is necessary for King to do this because he needs the clergymen to hear him, to listen to his argument that his actions were especially needed at this specific point in time. The clergymen would be forced to listen to King because he brought in the word of God to support himself; this is something that they have to follow no matter the circumstances. Essentially, he is forcing the clergymen to drop from their pedestal so King and them can be on a level playing field to have a balanced conversation. His adamancy in having the clergymen listen to him is due to the fact that the clergymen have a platform in which people are likely to listen. He wants to make sure that if the clergymen are educated, they can spread the word. It is necessary for the clergymen to listen to him because they would be hypocrites, wanting peace and love for everyone but not taking the steps to give that same peace to African Americans. Even if the clergymen did not want to do this in any way, they would be forced to because King’s word is backed by the lord’s word. As a consequence, the clergymen would more than likely hear out what Dr. King had to say, in that his actions in protesting were absolutely necessary under the command of God.

The Letter From Birmingham Jail text has been extremely relevant in the history following it. This text allowed King to make way for speeches, such as the “I Have a Dream” speech and leading him to be awarded a Nobel Peace Prize after the Civil Rights Act that was put into place in 1964. Unfortunately, because he was extremely successful in his activities, he was assassinated on April 4th, 1968. However, since King himself personified the civil rights movement, he has since been devoted to a holiday on the third Monday of every January to be memorialized in American history.
